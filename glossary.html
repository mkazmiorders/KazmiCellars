Wine Glossary

A beginner-friendly guide to the words you'll encounter on wine lists, labels, and tasting notes. No jargon without explanation — every term defined in plain English, with real-world examples to make it stick.

🔍

23 terms

Angel's Share Storytelling

The portion of wine or spirits that evaporates from a barrel during aging. In a typical wine cellar, a barrel can lose 2–5% of its volume each year to evaporation through the wood.

"The wine that quietly disappears into thin air while it's sleeping in the barrel."

Example: A winemaker aging Pinot Noir for 18 months might lose nearly a full bottle per barrel to the angel's share — a beautiful, poetic loss that concentrates what remains.

Related: Devil's Cut, Lees

Devil's Cut Storytelling

The portion of wine absorbed into the wood of the barrel during aging — the opposite of the Angel's Share. While the angel takes what evaporates, the devil keeps what soaks in.

"The wine the barrel itself drinks and holds onto, deep in the wood grain."

Example: When you crack open a used wine barrel, you can sometimes extract the devil's cut by adding water — it carries intense oak, vanilla, and caramel flavors.

Related: Angel's Share, Oaky

Terroir Storytelling

A French concept describing the complete natural environment where a wine is produced — the soil, climate, topography, and even the micro-organisms of a specific place — and how that environment expresses itself in the wine.

"The taste of where a wine was born. Two vineyards side by side can produce completely different wines because of terroir."

Example: A Pinot Noir from Burgundy's Chambolle-Musigny tastes different from one made a mile away in Gevrey-Chambertin — same grape, same producer, different terroir.

Related: Minerality, Vigneron

Cuvée Storytelling

A French term for a specific blend or batch of wine. It can refer to a single winemaker's flagship blend, a blend of multiple grape varieties, or a specific barrel selection — essentially the winemaker's intentional creation.

"A winemaker's chosen recipe — their considered blend, picked from everything they had available."

Example: A Champagne house might release a "Prestige Cuvée" — their finest blend of grapes from their best vineyards, representing the pinnacle of their craft.

Related: Vigneron, Balance

Lees Storytelling

The dead yeast cells and grape solids that settle at the bottom of a wine vessel after fermentation. Winemakers sometimes choose to age wine on its lees to add texture, richness, and complexity.

"The sediment left behind after fermentation — the yeast that did its job and settled to the bottom."

Example: Many fine Chardonnays and Champagnes are aged on their lees for months or years, giving them that distinctive creamy, bready texture.

Related: Sur Lie, Body

Sur Lie Storytelling

French for "on the lees." A winemaking technique where wine is left in contact with its lees for an extended period after fermentation. This adds richness, a creamy mouthfeel, and a subtle yeasty complexity to the finished wine.

"Letting the wine sit with its own sediment longer than usual — like steeping tea — to extract more flavor and texture."

Example: Muscadet from the Loire Valley is famous for its Sur Lie aging, which transforms a simple, acidic white wine into something with depth, saline character, and a slight effervescence.

Related: Lees, Body

Vigneron Storytelling

A French term for a person who cultivates a vineyard and makes wine from those grapes. Unlike a négociant (who buys grapes or wine from others), a vigneron controls the entire process from vine to bottle.

"A farmer-winemaker. Someone who grows the grapes and makes the wine themselves — soil to glass."

Example: The legendary Henri Jayer of Burgundy is considered one of the greatest vignerons of the 20th century — he farmed his plots with obsessive care and crafted wines that reshaped how the world thought about Pinot Noir.

Related: Terroir, Cuvée

Oenophile Storytelling

A person who loves and appreciates wine, often with a deep knowledge of viticulture, winemaking, and tasting. From the Greek "oinos" (wine) and "philos" (lover). Essentially: a serious wine enthusiast.

"A wine lover who goes beyond casual enjoyment — someone who studies, collects, and thinks deeply about wine."

Example: If you're reading a wine glossary at 10pm just to understand a label better, you might already be an oenophile and not know it yet.

Related: Terroir, Finish

Acidity Core Tasting

The natural tartness in wine that comes from acids present in grapes — primarily tartaric, malic, and citric acid. Acidity gives wine freshness, brightness, and the ability to age. It's what makes your mouth water after a sip.

"The zip and brightness you feel — like a squeeze of lemon. It's what makes wine feel alive and refreshing."

Example: A crisp Sauvignon Blanc from New Zealand has high acidity — it tastes fresh, almost electric, and pairs beautifully with seafood because that brightness cuts through richness.

Related: Crisp, Balance, Finish

Tannin Core Tasting

Natural compounds found in grape skins, seeds, and stems (and in oak barrels) that create a drying, gripping sensation in your mouth. Tannins are a key structural element in red wines and act as a natural preservative, allowing wines to age.

"That drying feeling on your gums and tongue after a sip of red wine — like strong black tea or the inside of a walnut skin."

Example: A young Barolo or Cabernet Sauvignon can feel almost harsh from tannins — but those same tannins soften beautifully with 10–20 years of aging, making the wine silky and complex.

Related: Structure, Body, Finish

Body Core Tasting

The weight and texture of wine in your mouth — how heavy or light it feels. Body is primarily determined by alcohol content, tannin, and residual sugar. Wines are generally described as light-, medium-, or full-bodied.

"The weight of the wine on your tongue. Compare it to the difference between skim milk (light), whole milk (medium), and cream (full-bodied)."

Example: Pinot Noir is typically light-bodied, Merlot is medium, and Cabernet Sauvignon or Syrah tend to be full-bodied — each feeling distinctly different in your mouth.

Related: Full-Bodied, Light-Bodied, Tannin

Finish Core Tasting

The taste and sensation that lingers in your mouth after you swallow a wine. A great wine's finish can last 30 seconds to several minutes. Length, complexity, and pleasantness of the finish are key quality indicators.

"What the wine says on its way out — and how long it keeps talking."

Example: An exceptional Burgundy might leave a finish of dried flowers, earth, and red fruit that lasts a full minute after swallowing. A simple table wine might vanish in seconds.

Related: Balance, Structure, Acidity

Balance Core Tasting

The harmony between a wine's key components — acidity, tannin, alcohol, sweetness, and fruit. A balanced wine has no single element dominating or standing out awkwardly. It is the most important quality marker in a fine wine.

"No one part is shouting louder than the others. Everything is in conversation — nothing is out of place."

Example: A wine with too much alcohol feels hot and burns. Too much acidity feels sharp and sour. When both are in balance with fruit and tannin, the wine feels effortless and elegant.

Related: Structure, Finish, Acidity

Structure Core Tasting

The framework of a wine — built from its acidity, tannin, alcohol, and sometimes sweetness. Structure is what gives a wine its shape, backbone, and potential to age. A wine without structure feels flat and formless.

"The skeleton of the wine. Just like a building needs a frame, a wine needs structure to hold everything together and stand the test of time."

Example: A young Napa Cabernet may taste almost too intense and grippy — but its strong structure means it will age magnificently for 20+ years as that framework softens.

Related: Tannin, Balance, Acidity

Dry Core Tasting

A wine with little to no residual sugar — meaning fermentation converted nearly all the grape sugars into alcohol. The opposite of sweet. Most red wines and many whites are dry. Dry does not mean lacking fruit flavor.

"Not sweet. The sugar was eaten up during fermentation — what's left is alcohol and flavor, not sweetness."

Example: A dry Cabernet Sauvignon can have rich blackcurrant and cherry aromas, but the actual taste isn't sweet — it's the absence of sugar that makes it feel savory and food-friendly.

Related: Off-Dry, Sweet, Tannin

Off-Dry Core Tasting

A wine with a small amount of residual sugar — not fully dry but not sweet either. The sugar is present but subtle, often balanced by high acidity so the wine doesn't taste overtly sweet.

"Just a whisper of sweetness — you might not even notice it consciously, but it rounds out the wine and makes it easier to drink."

Example: Many German Rieslings labeled "Kabinett" or "Spätlese" are off-dry — they have a touch of residual sugar that balances their piercing acidity, making them feel lush rather than tart.

Related: Dry, Sweet, Acidity

Sweet Core Tasting

A wine with significant residual sugar remaining after fermentation — either because fermentation was stopped early, or because very ripe or botrytized grapes were used. Sweetness ranges from gently honeyed to intensely syrupy.

"Sugar you can actually taste and feel — the wine coats your mouth with sweetness rather than just fruit flavor."

Example: Sauternes from Bordeaux is one of the world's great sweet wines — made from grapes affected by noble rot, it has flavors of honey, apricot, and marmalade with enough acidity to prevent it from feeling cloying.

Related: Dry, Off-Dry, Balance

Crisp Core Tasting

A tasting term describing a wine with noticeable, refreshing acidity that creates a clean, bright sensation on the palate. Crisp wines feel energetic and mouth-watering rather than flat or heavy. Used almost exclusively for white wines and sparkling wines.

"That snap of freshness — like biting into a cold, tart apple. It wakes your palate up."

Example: A well-made Pinot Grigio from Alto Adige or a Chablis Chardonnay is typically described as crisp — clean, fresh, with acidity that makes it the perfect aperitif wine.

Related: Acidity, Dry, Minerality

Jammy Core Tasting

A tasting descriptor for wines with very ripe, concentrated fruit flavors that evoke cooked or preserved fruit — like strawberry jam, blackberry compote, or plum preserve. Jammy wines often come from warm climates where grapes ripen fully.

"Fruit flavor that tastes cooked and concentrated — more like jam than fresh berries. Rich, thick, and sweet-smelling."

Example: Many big California Zinfandels and Australian Shirazes are described as jammy — they have intense, cooked berry flavors, high alcohol, and a plush, almost syrupy texture.

Related: Full-Bodied, Body, Sweet

Minerality Core Tasting

A tasting descriptor for flavors and sensations that evoke stones, slate, chalk, flint, or wet rock — non-fruit characteristics that seem to come from the soil itself. One of the most debated and evocative terms in wine description.

"The taste of the earth the vine grew in — like licking a wet stone or smelling the air just before rain."

Example: Great Chablis Chardonnay is famous for its minerality — a flinty, almost saline quality that has nothing to do with fruit and everything to do with the ancient oyster-shell limestone soils of the region.

Related: Terroir, Crisp, Acidity

Full-Bodied Core Tasting

A wine with a heavy, rich, and weighty presence on the palate — typically high in alcohol (13.5%+), tannin, and extract. Full-bodied wines feel substantial and chewy, coating the mouth with flavor and texture.

"Heavy and rich — like cream compared to water. You feel it filling your whole mouth."

Example: Napa Valley Cabernet Sauvignon, Châteauneuf-du-Pape, and Amarone are all full-bodied reds — dense, powerful wines that pair best with rich, hearty foods like red meat and aged cheeses.

Related: Body, Tannin, Light-Bodied

Light-Bodied Core Tasting

A wine with a lighter, more delicate presence on the palate — typically lower in alcohol (under 12.5%) and tannin, with a thinner texture. Light-bodied wines feel elegant and easy rather than heavy or dense.

"Light and airy — like water or skim milk on your tongue. Elegant, not heavy."

Example: Beaujolais, Pinot Noir from Burgundy, and Vinho Verde are typically light-bodied — they're bright, fresh, and versatile at the table, pairing easily with everything from salads to salmon.

Related: Body, Full-Bodied, Acidity

Oaky Core Tasting

A descriptor for wines that show prominent flavors derived from oak barrel aging — vanilla, toast, coconut, cedar, smoke, or baking spices. When well-integrated, oak adds complexity. When overdone, it can overwhelm the wine's natural character.

"That vanilla and toasty wood flavor you sometimes get — like smelling the inside of a wooden cabinet or a piece of toasted bread."

Example: A heavily oaked Chardonnay — often called "butter bomb" — might taste of vanilla cream, butter, and toasted oak. Compare that to an unoaked Chablis, which is lean, mineral, and purely about the grape.

Related: Devil's Cut, Balance, Full-Bodied
